# M813 Pre-Module Preparation Notes (Updated with Tutorial Insights)

## 1. Module Orientation

- **Length:** 6 months (~25 weeks, 300 hours study time)
- **Blocks:**
  - **Block 1 (Weeks 1–6):** Requirements & early SDLC (software development life cycle)
  - **Block 2 (Weeks 7–14):** Design, coding, patterns
  - **Block 3 (Weeks 15–20):** Architectures, integration
  - **Block 4 (Weeks 21–24):** EMA preparation and submission

- **Assessment:**
  - **3 TMAs** (tutor-marked assignments, one per main block, **all TMAs count**)
  - **1 EMA** (End-of-Module Assessment, no final exam, submitted online)
  - **Pass mark:** ≥50% in weighted TMA average **and** 50% in EMA; **all TMAs must be submitted** (no drop scores).

- **Learning Outcomes:**
  - Critically apply and evaluate current concepts, methods, and issues across the software development lifecycle.
  - Practically use UML and Java to model and implement (at prototype level) solutions to realistic organisational or enterprise problems.
  - Research, synthesise, and critically analyse cutting-edge literature in software development.
  - Communicate, reflect, and document findings clearly for an informed, academic or professional audience.
  - Develop research skills and the ability to track “leading edge” developments.

---

## 2. How You Will Study

- **Weekly:** Use the study planner for guided readings, practical work, and deadlines.
- **Self-directed research:** Look up unfamiliar topics in the OU Library and peer-reviewed sources.
- **Activities:** Engage in practicals (UML, coding in Java), applied to a chosen (ideally familiar) organisation, but *imaginary organisations are acceptable* (e.g., library systems).
- **Forum participation:** Introduce yourself, join module and “Tracking the Leading Edge” forums, and use specialist forums (private TMA/EMA discussion with your tutor).
- **Tutor contact:** Use your tutor for advice, TMA/EMA topic and case study support, and assessment guidance.

  - Skim contents, intro, and design principles summary.

### B. Academic & Study Skills

- Set up your notes system (e.g., OneNote, Notion, or folders by Block/Topic).
- Brush up on academic writing (critical, referenced, synthesis not summary).
- Practice using the OU Library to find and reference peer-reviewed articles.
- Prepare your forum introduction (background, interests, SE goals).
- Download and skim OU study skills resources (report writing, referencing, time management).

## 4. Subject Knowledge Primer

- **UML essentials:** Practice drawing use case, class, sequence, and activity diagrams in Visual Paradigm.
- **Java:** Be ready with OOP fundamentals—encapsulation, inheritance, polymorphism, interfaces vs abstract classes, exception handling.
- **Lifecycle:** Requirements gathering, analysis, design, implementation, testing, maintenance.
- **Design patterns:** Singleton, Observer, Factory, Strategy, Composite, MVC (see “Head First Design Patterns”).
- **Requirements engineering:** Stakeholder analysis, functional vs non-functional requirements, validation/verification.
- **Systems integration/architecture:** Components, services, layering, coupling/cohesion, enterprise application integration.
- **Configuration management:** Git/version control basics (concepts).
- **Academic practice:** Evidence-based, well-structured, clearly referenced written work.

---

## 5. Distinction-Level Study & Assignment Habits

- **Apply theory to practice:** Always support technical choices with references (module readings or peer-reviewed sources).
- **Critical evaluation:** Justify and compare methods (why this UML diagram, this pattern, this architecture?).
- **Synthesize, don’t summarize:** Connect concepts from readings, module, and your own experience.
- **Reflective log:** Regularly write short reflections on progress, choices, challenges, and how you’ll improve in future.
- **Use forums for “tracking the leading edge”:** Share recent advances or case studies you discover.

---

## 6. Practical & Assessment Insights from Tutorial

- **Choice of Organisation/Project:**  
  - Ideally select an organisation you know (current/past), or invent a realistic one (e.g. library, club, charity).  
  - Focus on *one particular aspect* (not a complete system)—show design/requirements/coding for a single “feature” or “problem area.”
- **Java is strongly recommended:**  
  - All module examples and test systems use Java. Non-Java code is possible but places you at a disadvantage and must have tutor agreement.
- **Assessment strategy:**  
  - TMAs: Part 1 (practical—80%), Part 2 (essay/critical evaluation—20%). TMA 3 may have a third, “leading edge” research component.
  - EMA: Research essay on a self-chosen topic, synthesising three recent, authoritative peer-reviewed articles. Topic guidance is provided near EMA time.
  - You may re-use an organisation/problem from previous OU postgrad modules (e.g., M816, M811).
- **Support & forums:**  
  - Tutor is your primary contact; use private forum for TMA/EMA advice and general discussion forum for module-wide questions.
  - Occasional drop-in Q&A sessions (not formal tutorials) are held for general TMA/EMA/assignment questions.
  - All key slides and recordings for tutorials will be posted on the forum.

## 8. Quick Reference: Core UML Diagrams

|------------|-----------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Use Case   | High-level system goals, actors         | Visual Paradigm |
| Class      | Structure: classes/attributes/relations | Visual Paradigm |
| Sequence   | Object interactions over time           | Visual Paradigm |

## 9. "Head First Design Patterns" Skim-Read Guide

- Focus on core principles:
  - "Design to interface, not implementation"
  - "Favor composition"
  - "Encapsulate what varies"
- For each pattern, know:
  - Applicability (when to use)
  - Structure (diagrams)
  - Advantages/disadvantages
